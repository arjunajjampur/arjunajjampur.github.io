THEA RENDER

Overview

Altair acquired Thea render in 2014 giving it access to industry leading visualization engine. Thea render is an award winning rendering and visualization product used by designers and architects.

One of the convergences after this acquisition was to bring in the capabilities of Thea into Inspire, our 3D modeling product.

My contribution

I was tasked with redesigning the post processing interface for what we called "Darkroom" within Inspire.

The goal was to make it easy to compare, re-condition and animate frames on rendered images.

Inconsistencies

The initial implementation after acquisition did not give product temas enough time to conduct UX research. This led to a less than desired state.

I started by taking a closer look at the UI and talking to several customers who were beta testers for this product. The findings were not surprising.

  1. The "Darkroom" UI did not feel like it was part of Altair family of products.
  2. IA was sometimes illogical and confusing. Checkboxes were sometimes on the right, and sometiems on the left.
  3. Iconography was inconsistent with other icons inside Inspire.
  4. Several post processing tools such as applying filters had a steep learning curve.

JOURNEY MAP

I then laid out the journey map for the entire post processing workflow which included several sub-flows such as render now,render later and relight (a way to change lighting after image is rendered).

The first rendition was a more traditional post-processing style with the main image in front-left, with tools on the right and a timeline at the bottom.

A/B testing

To better understand the accessibility of rendered and in-process image thumbnails, I had to redesign some image cards. These cards contained crucial information such as aspect ratio, type of rendering (full vs progressive) and current stage. We ran some tests in Maze to find the optimal solution with given set of constraints.

IMAGE GALLERY

I found some interesting ideas from using image filtering on my apple device and I proposed a way of pre-applying and visualizing filters on the right side of the UI

This reduced the time required to find the right filter by 70%.

RESULT

The new UI included several improvements:

  1. A new horizontal image gallery that shows both queued and completed image thumbnails.
  2. Queued images pre-rendered in mesh view to let users verify camera angle.
  3. Three distinct tabs for local rendering, relight and network rendering.
  4. Post processing panel with pre-applied filter presets.
  5. 13 new icons that align with our design system guidelines.

“The image gallery filtering is a game changer for us. I also love the quick preset for novice users” -- Dir. of PM, Renderinging technologies.
